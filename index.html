01 — About Me

Hi, I'm Ealena.

My research lives at the intersection of mathematics, physics, and machine learning. The central problem I'm obsessed with: how do you make a neural network that doesn't just extrapolate from data, but genuinely understands the physics of a problem?

I specialize in Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) — a method where partial differential equations are embedded directly into the neural training objective. The result is a model that respects conservation laws, even with very sparse data.

Alongside PINNs, I work with PiML pipelines, ensemble methods like Random Forests, and deep network architectures for applications ranging from cardiovascular blood flow to climate modeling and aerospace engineering.

What I love most: the inverse problem — using a handful of sensor measurements to recover hidden material properties that you can't directly measure. It feels like doing science with AI.

04 — Random Forest

Ensemble Methods

Random forests aren't just a baseline — for noisy experimental data and high-stakes predictions, their built-in uncertainty quantification is often the right tool.

1

Why I Use RF

When I have noisy sensor data from physical experiments, RF's ensemble averaging gives clean predictions without overfitting to individual measurements.

2

Variable Importance

Before training a PINN, I use RF feature importance to identify which input variables actually matter — cutting dimensionality before the expensive physics training.

3

Confidence Bands

Variance across trees gives free uncertainty estimates — I can flag predictions where the model is uncertain before feeding them into a safety decision.

4

Stochastic PDE Params

RF surrogate models let me rapidly scan parameter space for stochastic PDEs — running the full PINN only on the most interesting regions.

05 — Physics-Informed ML

PiML

The idea is simple but powerful: if you already know the physics, encode it. The network learns to be physically consistent, not just statistically accurate.

1

Identify the Physics

Choose the governing PDE for your system — this becomes the physics regularizer during training.

2

Build the Loss

Total loss = data fidelity + physics residual. Weighting λ controls how much the model trusts data vs physics.

3

Sample Collocation Points

Adaptively sample points throughout the domain where PDE residuals will be minimized during training.

4

Fuse Data Sources

Combine cheap simulation data with sparse, expensive experimental measurements — both contribute to learning.

5

Validate with Physics

Beyond test error — check conservation law compliance, mass balance, energy conservation as validation metrics.
